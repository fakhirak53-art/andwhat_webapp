Card 1 – Bullying, Safety & Boundaries 

Q. Lately, how safe and respected do you feel around other people (in person or online)? 

A. I'm being bullied and it's really getting to me 
B. Some stuff happens that upsets me, but I'm not sure if it's bullying 
C. I'm not being bullied, but I see it happening to other people 
D. I feel pretty safe and respected most of the time 

 

If A: “I'm being bullied and it's really getting to me” 

Answer: 
Being bullied is like having a glitch in your personal operating system that keeps crashing your day. It's not okay, and you don't deserve to feel like you're constantly in a bad Wi-Fi zone. 

Support message: 
You're not overreacting, and this isn't "just drama." If you can, talk to a trusted adult – like a teacher, year level coordinator, school counsellor, or family member. Think of them as your personal IT support. Explain what's been happening, even if it comes out as a frustrated rant. If it feels safe, keep receipts: screenshots, messages, dates, places. If you ever feel in immediate danger, contact emergency services or a crisis line. You don't have to fight this boss battle alone; getting help is like calling in a squad. 

Shape 

If B: “Some stuff happens that upsets me, but I'm not sure if it's bullying” 

Answer: 
If someone's actions are consistently making you feel like you're walking on emotional LEGOs, it's worth taking seriously. You don't need an official "Bullying" stamp to know it sucks. 

Support message: 
Trust your gut feeling – it's usually right. Bullying isn't always a cartoon villain pushing you into a locker. It can be subtle: constant "jokes" that aren't funny, being excluded from the group chat, or weird passive-aggressive comments. You don't have to wait until it's a full-blown crisis to ask for help. You could tell a school counsellor or trusted adult, "Some things are happening that make me uncomfortable, and I'm not sure what to call it." They can help you decode the situation and figure out your next move. 

Shape 

If C: “I'm not being bullied, but I see it happening to other people” 

Answer: 
You've got that "Spidey-sense" for injustice. Noticing when someone else is getting a raw deal is a superpower, even if you don't wear a cape. 

Support message: 
You don't have to confront the bully like a superhero in a movie. Small actions can make a huge difference. Check in with the person: "Hey, I saw what happened. Are you okay?" or "That wasn't cool, you didn't deserve that." You can also quietly report what you've seen to a trusted adult. Yeah, the "snitch" label sucks, but protecting someone from ongoing crap is way more important than what some people might think. Be the plot twist in someone's bad day. 

Shape 

If D: “I feel pretty safe and respected most of the time” 

Answer: 
You've basically achieved "optimal human experience" status. Feeling safe and respected is like having good Wi-Fi for your brain – everything just runs smoother. 

Support message (humour version): 
That's awesome! A safe and respectful environment is crucial for your mental health, confidence, and not wanting to throw your phone across the room. Keep paying attention to how people treat you and others. If anything ever changes, you're allowed to speak up early – no need to wait for a full-blown drama episode. You can also help keep your school safe by being inclusive, using humour that doesn't punch down, and standing up for those who are being picked on. Your chill vibe can make a huge difference.
